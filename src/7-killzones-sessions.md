# 7. Killzones and Sessions

Not all trading hours are created equal. The giants don't work 24 hours a day — they operate in concentrated bursts during specific windows when liquidity is deepest and price discovery is most active. These windows are called **killzones**, and knowing them is the difference between trading when the giants are active and trading when they're not.

A setup that would print money at 9:30 AM New York can produce pure noise at 3:00 AM New York. Same chart, same structure, different context.

## Concepts

### The three major sessions

All ICT timing is typically expressed in **New York time** (America/New_York). The three global sessions are:

| Session | Approx. NY time | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **Asia** | 7:00 PM – 3:00 AM | Quiet, ranging — typically accumulation |
| **London** | 2:00 AM – 11:00 AM | High volatility — manipulation + early distribution |
| **New York** | 7:00 AM – 4:00 PM | Highest volume — main directional moves |

London and New York overlap from roughly 7:00 AM – 11:00 AM NY time — this is the busiest window of the trading day for most instruments.

### The four killzones

Within those sessions, ICT identifies four specific high-probability windows:

1. **Asian Killzone** — 7:00 PM – 10:00 PM NY
   - Accumulation in progress
   - Watch the Asian range highs and lows — they become liquidity targets for London
   - Don't expect big moves here; expect *setup*

2. **London Killzone** — 2:00 AM – 5:00 AM NY
   - The judas swing typically fires in this window
   - Expect a sweep of the Asian range (up or down)
   - This is where the day's manipulation phase usually happens

3. **New York AM Killzone** — 8:30 AM – 11:00 AM NY
   - The main distribution move
   - Highest-probability window for directional setups
   - 8:30 AM is the US equity open and major economic releases — often the catalyst

4. **New York PM / Close Killzone** — 1:30 PM – 4:00 PM NY
   - Secondary opportunity — either continuation of the AM move or reversal
   - Less reliable than the AM window
   - End-of-day positioning creates clean setups into the close

### Why killzones work

Liquidity concentrates at specific times because:

- **Banks desk-hand-offs** happen at session changes — Tokyo hands to London, London hands to New York. Each transition brings a fresh burst of institutional flow.
- **Economic releases** cluster in the morning windows (London AM, NY AM) — these are when funds update positioning.
- **Equity market opens and closes** (LSE 3:00 AM NY, NYSE 9:30 AM NY, etc.) drive correlated flow across FX, indices, metals.

The giants need other giants on the other side of their trades. The killzones are when the other giants are at their desks.

### The Asian range

The high and low set during the Asian session are **critical liquidity levels** for London. Every London session typically starts with a sweep of one (sometimes both) of these levels.

A simple playbook:
1. Mark the Asian range high and low
2. Wait for London to sweep one of them (with displacement)
3. Trade in the *opposite direction* — the sweep was the manipulation

This is the purest form of the judas swing play.

### The NY midnight open

The **midnight NY open** (0:00 NY time) is a reference price used by many ICT traders. It often acts as the day's equilibrium — price above it tends bullish for the day, price below it tends bearish.

It's not magical; it's just a commonly-watched level that institutional algos use as an intraday anchor.

### Killzones and instruments

Killzones aren't universal — they depend on what you're trading:

- **FX (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY)** — London + NY killzones are king
- **Indices (ES, NQ, DAX)** — NY AM killzone is by far the most reliable (US equity hours)
- **Commodities (Gold, Oil)** — London open + NY AM
- **Crypto** — 24/7, but still respects the NY/London rhythm surprisingly well

Know when *your* instrument's giants are active, and trade then.

### Day of week matters too

- **Monday** — often continuation of Friday's close; can be quiet early
- **Tuesday–Wednesday** — highest-probability directional days
- **Thursday** — often the weekly high/low is set here (position-squaring before Friday)
- **Friday** — early moves fade, end-of-day often reverses into the weekend

This isn't a rule, but it's a strong tendency. Weekly range analysis becomes more reliable when you know which day is likely to deliver the weekly high or low.

### Combining killzones with PO3

The best intraday setups stack killzones with Power of 3 (Chapter 6):

- **Asian killzone** = accumulation
- **London killzone** = manipulation (judas swing)
- **NY AM killzone** = distribution (the real move)

If you see a clean London sweep during the London killzone, and you're entering on confirmation during the NY AM killzone, you're lined up with the institutional rhythm. That's the edge compounding.

### Watch out: trading the dead zones

Outside killzones, liquidity thins out and price drifts randomly. Setups that look identical in both contexts are dramatically lower probability during dead zones. The common dead zones are:

- **NY lunch** (11:30 AM – 1:00 PM NY) — everyone steps away from the desk
- **Late Asia** (3:00 AM – 5:00 AM NY, after London opens but before the big moves) — wait
- **Friday afternoon** — positioning winds down

When in doubt, wait for a killzone.

### Watch out: killzone ≠ guaranteed move

A killzone is a window of *elevated probability*, not a guarantee. Some London sessions are quiet. Some NY AMs drift sideways. Don't force a trade just because it's "killzone time" — wait for the actual setup to materialise inside the window.

### Watch out: daylight savings shifts the clock

Killzone times are tied to New York local time, which observes daylight savings. If you're in a region that doesn't (or shifts on different dates), your broker clock will drift relative to NY time for part of the year. Double-check your killzone times in March and November.
