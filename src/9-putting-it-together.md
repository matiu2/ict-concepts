# 9. Putting It Together

You now have the full vocabulary: structure, liquidity, order blocks, imbalances, premium/discount, displacement, the Power of 3, killzones, and OTE. On their own, each concept is interesting. Stacked, they become a complete method for reading what the giants are doing and trading with the edge.

This chapter is the assembly manual. It's how a working ICT trader walks up to a chart each day and decides whether there's a trade, where to enter, and where to get out.

## The daily workflow

### Step 1 — Higher-timeframe bias

Always start with the biggest picture and work down. The daily, 4-hour, and 1-hour charts tell you the *direction* of the story the giants are telling this week.

Ask:

1. What's the **daily structure**? HH + HL + BOS, or LL + LH + BOS, or ranging?
2. What's the **most recent daily CHoCH** — has it been followed by a new trend or reclaimed?
3. Where is price relative to the **weekly dealing range**? Premium or discount?
4. What's the **next untouched liquidity pool** above and below current price?

You should come out of this with a one-sentence bias: *"I'm looking for longs from discount into the buy-side pool at [price]."* If you can't say that, don't trade — the day has no story yet.

### Step 2 — Intermediate timeframe structure

Drop to the H1 (or whatever your "middle" timeframe is). Confirm the daily bias is still intact there, and identify:

- The current dealing range you'll measure OTE from
- The most recent unmitigated order block in your bias direction
- Any FVGs between current price and your target

### Step 3 — Timing

Check the clock.

- Is a relevant **killzone** coming up?
- Are there any **economic releases** that could create the manipulation leg?
- What phase of the **Power of 3** is the day currently in?

If the answer is "we're mid-NY lunch, Asia is done, no events for hours" — step away. Trade when the giants are at their desks.

### Step 4 — Entry refinement

Drop to your execution timeframe (M5/M15 typically) and look for the specific setup:

- Price approaching or inside your **OTE zone** (62%–79% of the relevant leg)
- **Liquidity sweep** on the way in (bonus confluence)
- Reaction off an **OB or FVG** inside the OTE
- **LTF CHoCH** confirming the reversal (optional if confluence is strong)

### Step 5 — Execute

- **Entry** — at the confluence zone
- **Stop** — beyond the swing that defines the setup, with a small buffer
- **Target** — next major liquidity pool or structural level (2R+ minimum)

Size the position so the stop is a fixed percentage of your account (typically 0.5%–1% per trade). This is the non-negotiable survival rule — every other concept in this book is useless if a few losing trades can blow up the account.

## The decision checklist

Before clicking buy or sell, walk through this:

- [ ] Is my **HTF bias** clear and still in effect?
- [ ] Am I in the **correct half of the dealing range** (discount for longs, premium for shorts)?
- [ ] Did price **sweep liquidity** on the way into my zone?
- [ ] Is there an **OB or FVG** at my entry?
- [ ] Did the move *to* my zone show **displacement** (strong candles, BOS)?
- [ ] Am I entering during a **killzone** or in dead time?
- [ ] Does my **stop** sit beyond a structural point, not a random price?
- [ ] Is my **target** at meaningful liquidity, with at least 2R available?

Four or more "yes" answers = the setup has real edge. Two or three = marginal, consider skipping. Zero or one = walk away.

## Common ways this goes wrong

### Over-layering timeframes

Some traders use seven timeframes for every trade. This creates analysis paralysis. You need *three*:
- **Bias timeframe** (daily or H4)
- **Structure timeframe** (H1 or M15)
- **Entry timeframe** (M5 or M1)

Pick one of each, use them consistently, and ignore the rest.

### Fighting the HTF

The single most common mistake: spotting a "perfect" LTF setup against the HTF trend. These setups fail at a much higher rate than the same setup *with* the HTF. The giants are pushing the HTF direction — your LTF trade is a raft against the current.

If the HTF says long, take only longs. Missing trades in the other direction isn't a problem — it's discipline.

### Entering without confirmation in low-confluence setups

A setup with only 2 out of 8 boxes ticked *can* still work, but it needs confirmation. Waiting for an LTF CHoCH before entering adds a stage-3 confirmation and converts a marginal setup into a reasonable one.

High-confluence setups (6+ boxes) can often be taken as limit orders. Low-confluence setups need the trigger.

### Revenge trading after a miss

The feeling of watching a perfect setup play out without you is painful. The response — chasing a late entry, or taking the next half-baked setup — is how most accounts die. The market will print thousands of setups this year. You don't need this one.

### Moving stops

The stop is where your thesis is invalidated. If you move the stop further away because "I'm sure it'll come back," you're no longer trading a plan — you're hoping. Stops go only one direction: *to* break-even or further in profit, never further away.

## The mindset

Three principles, in order:

### 1. Every trade is a bet

No setup, no matter how textbook, is a guarantee. You are not predicting the future. You are taking calculated bets where the odds are tilted in your favour.

### 2. The edge is in the aggregate

A single trade tells you nothing. 50 trades tells you whether your process has an edge. 500 trades tells you the size of it. Individual outcomes are noise — only the pattern across many trades reveals the truth.

This means: **you will lose trades that "should have won."** You will win trades that "should have lost." Don't let individual results convince you to change the process. Measure the process, not the trade.

### 3. Patience compounds

The giants don't care if they make a trade today. Neither should you. Sitting out 90% of the market, and entering only when the full stack lines up, is how professional traders outperform. Most of the work is *not* trading.

Discipline beats analysis. Analysis beats instinct. Instinct — unaided by the other two — loses.

## Where to go from here

This book has laid out the core ICT concepts in a logical learning order. From here, the work is in three places:

### Repetition on live charts

Pull up a clean chart (daily, then H1, then M5) and walk through the checklist. Do this every day. The concepts only become intuitive after hundreds of annotated charts.

### Backtesting

Take a single setup type — say, "OTE from a discount OB during the NY AM killzone with a London sweep" — and backtest 50 of them across multiple instruments. Measure win rate, average R, and how often the full stack actually appears. This tells you whether your interpretation of the concepts matches what actually happens in price.

### Journaling real trades

Every live trade goes in a journal. Record the setup, the confluence stack, the reasoning, the outcome, and — most importantly — what you'd do differently. The gap between "I know this" and "I trade this" only closes through reflection on real results.

---

The giants will keep doing what they do. They need liquidity; they build in discount and distribute in premium; they leave footprints in the candles whether anyone is watching or not. Your job is just to read the story carefully, spot the clues, and bet with the edge you've built.

Be patient, be disciplined, and trust the aggregate. The rest takes care of itself.
